4.3. Product-to-sum rules¶

Two useful trigonometric identities are the “product-to-sum” formulae, for angles \(\theta\) and \(\phi\):

(4.2)¶\[\cos\left(\theta\right) \cdot \cos\left(\phi\right) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(\cos\left(\theta + \phi\right) + \cos\left(\theta - \phi\right) \right)\]

and

(4.3)¶\[\cos\left(\theta\right) \cdot \sin\left(\phi\right) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(\sin\left(\theta + \phi\right) - \sin\left(\theta - \phi\right) \right)\]

These rules are primarily used when comparing two sinusoids by the multiply-and-add method. These rules also have an interesting interpretation when applied to audio signals: loosely speaking, they say that if you multiply two pure tones with frequencies \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) together (sample by sample), the resulting signal is the average of two pure tones, one at frequency \(f_1 + f_2\) and the other at frequency \(f_1 - f_2\).

4.4. Complex exponentials¶

Euler’s formula (3.10) gives us the identity

\[ e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot\theta} = \cos(\theta) + \mathrm{j} \cdot\sin(\theta). \]

By combining this with the symmetry rules for waves, we can recover definitions of \(\cos\) and \(\sin\) in terms of the exponential:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \cos(\theta) &= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot \theta} + e^{-\mathrm{j}\cdot\theta}\right)\\ \sin(\theta) &= \frac{1}{2\mathrm{j}} \cdot \left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot \theta} - e^{-\mathrm{j}\cdot\theta}\right)\\ \end{align*} \end{split}\]

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} \cos(\theta) \cdot \cos(\phi) &=\frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot \theta} + e^{-\mathrm{j}\cdot\theta}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot \phi} + e^{-\mathrm{j}\cdot\phi}\right) & \text{definition}\\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot \left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta + \phi)} + e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta - \phi)} + e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(-\theta + \phi)} + e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(-\theta - \phi)}\right) & \text{FOIL multiply}\\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot \left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta + \phi)} + e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(-\theta - \phi)} + e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta - \phi)} + e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(-\theta + \phi)} \right) & \text{rearrange terms}\\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot \left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta + \phi)} + e^{-\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta + \phi)} + e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta - \phi)} + e^{-\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta - \phi)} \right) & \text{factor out } -1\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(\frac{1}{2}\cdot\left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta + \phi)} + e^{-\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta + \phi)}\right) + \frac{1}{2}\cdot\left(e^{\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta - \phi)} + e^{-\mathrm{j}\cdot(\theta - \phi)}\right) \right) & \text{group terms}\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(\cos\left(\theta + \phi\right) + \cos\left(\theta - \phi\right) \right). \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Some students find the geometric approach more intuitive, and some find the algebraic approach more intuitive. Neither is necessarily better than the other, so use whichever works for you!
